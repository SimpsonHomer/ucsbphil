Philosophy 1

Short Introduction to Philosophy

CHEM 1171 | MWF 9:00-9:50 + Discussion

We will discuss the questions: Can we know anything? Is the mind a physical thing? Can the existence of God somehow be proved? How can the will be free if we are part of the natural order studied by science? Why are you and the child from which you grew the same person?

Required Text(s):
Descartes, Meditations on First Philosophy
Perry, Dialogue on Personal Identity and Immortality
Taylor, Metaphysics

Instructor: A. Brueckner

Philosophy 3

Critical Thinking

GIRV 1004 | MWF 10:00-10:50 + Discussion

If you take this class, then you will learn to think critically. Therefore, if you do not take this class, then you will not learn to think critically. You do wish to learn to think critically. So, you should take this class. Convinced? You shouldn't be. The previous paragraph is an example of bad reasoning. Why? Take this class and find out. The primary topic of concern in this class will be arguments. Along the way, numerous concepts (e.g. validity, soundness, deduction/induction) will be introduced to further the analysis. Informal fallacies will be covered and students will be introduced to a formal system of propositional logic. Dealing with this formal system will occupy a fair amount of our time. Methods for assessing arguments containing categorical statements, statements relating two classes, will also be introduced. Anyone interested in correct reasoning is strongly encouraged to take this class. This class falls under the World Civilization and Thought GE requirement as well. As if that wasn't enough incentive, this class will also benefit those students planning on taking such standardized tests as the GRE or LSAT.

Required Text(s):
Hurley, Concise Introduction to Logic, 8th edition

Instructor: C. Buford

Philosophy 4

Introduction to Ethics

PHELPS 1260 | MWF 12:00-12:50 + Discussion

In this class, we will survey the modern ethical territory by examining issues in applied ethics, theoretical ethics, and the challenges of environmentalism, feminism, and multiculturalism to ethics. In finishing this class, students should have a good idea of the basic metaethical and normative positions in ethics and what these entail practically. Students should leave class fairly confident of what ethical views they prefer, as well. Here are some of the questions we will explore in the first part of the class: Is death bad? Are people who 'luck out' and receive little or no consequences for their 'immoral' actions less morally culpable? Under what circumstances is war morally permissible? What is good about equality and is it permissible for people to prefer certain individuals over others based on race or gender? In the second part of the class, we will explore issues in ethical theory such as: In what sense are people who lack concern for others a challenge to ethics? Is ethics subjective or relative? What does 'good' mean? In the third segment of the class, we will explore the biases upon which we have formulated many of our answers to ethical dilemmas. We will examine the challenges of environmentalism, feminism, and multiculturalism to ethical theory and seek a theory which is well-informed. The coursework will consist of three objective exams and three papers.

Required Text(s):
Nagel, Mortal Questions
Sterba, Three Challenges to Ethics
Williams, Morality: An Introduction to Ethics

Instructor: H. Salazar

Philosophy 6

Professional and Business Ethics

WEBB 1100 | MWF 1:00-1:50 + Discussion

This course will investigate ethical issues that confront those in business, medicine, and the law. Business and professional ethics have recently begun to attract more interest and courses on ethics are required at nearly all business, medical, and law schools. This course has been designed to give students exposure to many of the important issues in these fields and to give them a head start on issues that they will encounter in business and professional schools. The course selections focus mainly on issues that face individual business people, doctors, and lawyers and not on broad subjects that are more institutional in scope (e.g., can doctors lie to patients rather than is our legal system fair?). The course will be broken down into the following three units, wherein the following issues are likely to be addressed:
1) Business ethics: Is affirmative action morally permissible? What kinds of contracts, if any, are unconscionable and unenforceable? Is it morally permissible for people to bluff during negotiations? Do companies have obligations other than maximize profits? What is the moral status of sweatshops in third-world countries? Is "whistle blowing" permissible or does it violate fiduciary relationships between employees and employers? What is the moral status of drug testing of employees?
2) Medical ethics: The Hippocratic Oath. What are the moral obligations of the doctor? Is it permissible for doctors to lie to patients? To withhold the truth? What limits, if any, should be placed on patient autonomy? Is there any scope for paternalism within considerations of autonomy? Who determines patient competence? What is the moral status of informed consent? How does the introduction of HMOs change the obligations of the doctor?
3) Ethics of lawyering: What are the obligations of the lawyer? Which takes priority, considerations of justice or the importance of "zealous advocacy?" Is it morally permissible for a defense lawyer to put witnesses on the stand who he expects to commit perjury? Can he encourage perjurious testimony? Should the "last lawyer in town" be forced to represent a client in need?
Students will be evaluated primarily based on a midterm exam, final exam, and final paper. Section attendance and participation will also be considered. The course will use a reader which will be available at GrafikArt in Isla Vista. For more information, email the instructor at fritz88777@aol.com.

Required Text(s):
Reader

Instructor: F. Allhoff

Philosophy 20C

History of Philosophy

BUCHN 1910 | MWF 11:00-11:50 + Discussion

What does THE WORLD have to be like if human beings are to make contact with it? Alternatively, what does THE MIND have to be like to make contact with the world? Call these two related questions The Dominating Questions. Philosophy 20C is the third course of a series that devotes itself to the study of philosophical works written during the modern period (1600-1800). In this course, we will study the work of four or five philosophers that dominate the period: Locke, Berkeley, Hume, Reid and Kant. Although each of these philosophers made contributions to various sub-disciplines of philosophy, in this course we will study the work with a view of grasping what each has to say in regards to The Dominating Questions. That is, we shall study the work of these philosophers with respect to their theories of cognition.

Required Text(s):
Reid, Inquiry and Essays
The Empiricists
Reader

Instructor: M. Fletcher

Philosophy 138

Normative Ethics

BUILDING 387 ROOM 104 | MWF 11:00-11:50

We humans are able to think about what to do and able to govern ourselves by the results of that thinking. We can, as many philosophers have put it, "reason practically." There are some kinds of reasons that we expect each person to take into account, even when doing so would not advance that person's own aims. These kinds of reasons are typically identified as "moral" or "ethical" reasons. Many philosophers think that there are two categories of moral or ethical reasons: We're expected to take into account the ways in which our acts are good or bad for those who are affected by them, i.e., considerations of welfare; and, we're expected to avoid "wronging" those who are affected by what we do, i.e., considerations of "justice" or "rights". The course will focus on contemporary and recent work on the nature and source of this second category of ethical reasons. We'll begin with an examination of Consequentialist accounts, and then read some criticisms of Consequentialism that should shed light on this part of ethics. Then, we'll focus on some non-Consequentialist accounts. A reader will be available that will include work by Nagel, Sen, Scanlon, Gewirth, Thompson, and others. Philosophy 4 or 100A (or a comparable Introduction to Ethics course) is recommended. Work for the course will include one long (about 10 page) paper that I'll help you develop through the quarter, one cumulative final, and regular attendance and participation in the lecture/discussion that will include regular short assignments to help you keep up with the readings and prepare for discussion.
Before you come to the first class meeting, you should check out these web-links from James Pryor's site. They should be very helpful for everyone, even those who have more experience reading and writing philosophy.
http://www.princeton.edu/~jimpryor/general/reading.html
http://www.princeton.edu/~jimpryor/general/writing.html
http://www.princeton.edu/~jimpryor/general/vocab/

Required Text(s):
Reader

Instructor: D. Senestraro

Philosophy 150B/250B

Advanced Topics in the Theory of Knowledge

HSSB 3201, PHELPS 1448 | TR 11:00-12:15

In this course we will examine some recent work on the problem of skepticism about the external world. We will consider a variety of attempts to "answer the skeptic," including relevant alternatives accounts, contextualism, and arguments based on externalism about mental content. We will also consider the possibility that skepticism is a problem we will always have with us.

Required Text(s):
Derose, Skepticism: A Contemporary Reader

Instructor: K. Falvey

Philosophy 150D/250D

Advanced Topics in the Philosophy of Mind

HSSB 1227 | TR 2:00-3:15

What sort of properties are colors? Are colors intrinsic properties of objects, or is color just an artefact of the way the human mind perceives certain features of the world? In this course we will examine a number of different philosophical accounts of the nature of color. Some attention will be given to the physics of color and the physiology of color perception. Readings will be from early modern and 20th century sources.

Required Text(s):
Reader
Recommended Text: Wittgenstein, Remarks on Color

Instructor: K. Falvey

Philosophy 152

Plato

ARTS 1251 | TR 12:30-1:45

Note: Philosophy 252G is canceled. Plato's early dialogues and the Republic. Topics will include methodological issues such as Socratic definition and the Socratic method; the moral views of the Platonic Socrates, including Socratic paradoxes, Socratic intellectualism, and Socrates' objections to traditional rhetoric and sophistry; and Plato's metaphysics and epistemology as developed in the middle books of the Republic . A principal goal of the course will be to locate the epistemological and ethical issues raised by these dialogues in the context of contemporary philosophical discussion.

Required Text(s):
Annas, An Introduction to Plato's Republic. Cooper, Plato: Complete Works. Guthrie, Socrates

Instructor: R. Glatz

Philosophy 160/260G

Descartes

GIRV 2120 | TR 2:00-3:15

It has been said that much of 20th century philosophy can be seen as an attempt to break the hold of Descartes on our philosophical sensibilities. Whatever the truth of that, there can be no doubt of Descartes' enormous influence on the agenda of philosophy in this century--e.g., the centrality of epistemology to philosophy, the obsession with skepticism, the mind/body problem (or problems), the nature of the self. Perhaps no other past philosopher except Hume is still so much with us. In this course we will examine Descartes' conception of the task of philosophy as well as his epistemology, metaphysics and philosophy of the mind.

Required Text(s):
Descartes, The Philosophical Writings, Volumes 1 and 2

Instructor: J.W. Forgie

Philosophy 185/285G

Advanced Modern/Mathematical Logic

SH 5617 | MWF 10:00-10:50

We begin with an informal exposition and philosophical discussion of some the results of modern logic: Church's Theorem and Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems. The precise explanation and rigorous proof of the first of these will then follow. Along the way we explain the idea of a "Turing Machine", an abstract kind of machine (extremely stupid but) capable of an amazing variety of computational tasks. The conception of a Turing Machine has been important in recent discussions in the philosophy of mind and in the philosophy of science. During the second five weeks of the course we will be concerned to explain and sketch proofs of Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems and Tarski's theorem about the undefinability of truth. If time permits, we will consider some facts about second-order logic. Course grades will be based on homework (2/7), a take-home midterm examination (2/7), and a final examination (3/7).

Required Text(s):
Boolos, Burgess and Jeffrey, Computability and Logic, 4th edition

Instructor: C.A. Anderson

Philosophy 296E

Seminar in Metaphysics

SH 5617 | T 3:30-6:20

We will look closely at an argument, due independently to Alonzo Church and Kurt Gödel, and perhaps ultimately to Frege, that the denotation (referent, designatum) of a sentence is its truth value. Related issues concerning the nature of truth and of facts will also be discussed. We will begin with a careful reading of Stephen Neale's book, Facing Facts.

Required Text(s):
Kirkham, Theories of Truth: A Concise Introduction
Lynch, The Nature of Truth
Neale, Facing Facts
Schmitt, Truth: A Primer

Instructor: N. Salmon

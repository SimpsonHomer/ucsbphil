Philosophy 3

Critical Thinking

BUCHN 1920 | MWF (10:00-10:50am) + Discussion

Phil 3 is an introductory course in logic and critical thinking. Specifically, logic is the discipline concerned with correct reasoning and the quality of arguments (whether they are good or bad). The ability to reason is an integral part of any college education as it prepares you to assess, and not be fooled by, the many arguments you will be confronted with in your everyday life (e.g., by politicians, the media, your peers). Phil 3 will also help you in taking standardized tests (like the LSAT and GRE) that test your analytic skills. Anyone interested in learning how to reason well is highly encouraged to take this class.

Required Text(s):
Patrick Hurley, A Concise Introduction to Logic 8th Ed.

Instructor: Carl Barnes | cbarne00@umail.ucsb.edu

Course TAs: Chandler | Way

Philosophy 20C

History of Philosophy: Empiricists to Kant

BUCH 1910 | MWF (11:00-11:50am) + Discussion

This is the third and final course in the PHIL 20 series of classes surveying the history of philosophy in the West. In this last course, we examine the main developments in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries: the so-called early modern period. (Note that you do not have to have taken 20A or 20B in order to enroll for 20C.) We will examine the work of four major figures in the period: John Locke (1632-1704), George Berkeley (1685-1753), David Hume (1711-1776) and Immanuel Kant (1724-1804). The focus of the course will be the epistemological and metaphysical systems developed by these pivotal thinkers, and in particular their various theories of the relationship between mind and world. Reading will consist largely in representative selections from each of these four philosophers.

Philosophy 134/234G

Moral Psychology

PHELP 3505 | TR (12:30-1:45pm)

Moral psychology is an attempt to understand virtue and evil, praise and blame, pride and humility, freedom and responsibility, practical deliberation and action. To explain these phenomena the moral psychologist must answer a number of historically persistent, thorny questions about the nature of action and evaluation. In this course we will concentrate on three different characters 'the altruist, the amoralist, and the addict'and the light they throw on some of these questions. The questions we will ask include: Is altruism possible? If it is not, why can't we act for the sake of others? If it is possible, does this mean we can act on something other than our desires? Are there amoralists? Can someone have an entirely correct set of moral views (judgments or opinions) while having no motivation to act morally? If so, does this show that moral judgments are not intrinsically motivating? What is addiction, and how does it relate to more ordinary forms of temptation and weakness of will? What is it to resist a desire or give into temptation? Is it ever irrational to yield to one's strongest desire? If acting on one's strongest desire can be irrational, when is it, and why? When are people responsible for some moral or immoral action? When is it appropriate to praise or blame someone for what she has done? If its initial state and the laws of physics fully determined the future state of the universe, could we freely decide how to act? If free decisions and actions are compatible with the truth of determinism, when, if ever, is a decision or action not free? We will look for answers to these questions in the work of Michael Bratman, James Drier, Harry Frankfurt, Christine Korsgaard, Elijah Millgram, John Robertson, Peter Strawson, Peter van Inwagen, Gary Watson, David Wiggins, Bernard Williams and perhaps others. Requirements for the course consist in two medium length papers, both of which must be rewritten in response to comments. Additional shorter assignments are possible as well.

Required Text(s):
Course Reader

Instructor: Aaron Zimmerman | azimmerman@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Philosophy 144/244G

Advanced Philosophy of Law

HSSB 1232 | TR (2:00-3:15pm)

The course will focus primarily upon the philosophical foundations of criminal law. Topics discussed will include: the actus reus (guilty act) and mens rea (guilty mind) conditions for criminal liablity; attempt, conspiracy, and other 'inchoate' offenses; the distinction between justification and excuse; and such particluar defenses as necessity, duress, and self-defense. We may also discuss the relationship between criminal law and civil law, especially tort law.

Required Text(s):
George Fletcher, Basic Concepts of Criminal Law (Oxford U. Press)

Philosophy 162/262G

Leibniz

NH 1105 | MWF (1:00-1:50pm)

This course will examine the philosophy of the seventeenth-century rationalist Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716). We will concentrate on understanding his metaphysical system, including his views on truth, necessity and possibility, substance, causation and freedom. We will focus primarily on reading Leibnizâ€™s original texts rather than commentaries. Our aim will be to understand his theories, to appreciate their strengths and track down their weaknesses, and to consider how some of his ideas played out (and continue to play out) in later philosophical epochs.

Note: Students should appreciate that Leibniz is one of the more difficult of early modern philosophers. It is recommended (though not required) that students have some background in other early modern philosophers such as Descartes, Spinoza, Locke, or Kant at an upper division level before approaching Leibniz.

Required Text(s):
Rescher, G.W. Leibniz's Monadology: An Edition for Students 1st edition. Univ. of Pittsburgh
Leibniz, Philosophical Essays 1st edition. Hackett.

Instructor: Thomas Holden | tholden@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Philosophy 188/288G

Value Theory

BLDG 387 #104 | T (3:30-6:20pm)

This course focuses on the moral and legal principles underlying the protection of human rights in the European system, with particular emphasis on the famed 'margin of appreciation doctrine' of the jurisprudence of the European Court of Human Rights. Although not without faults, the European system is hailed as the world's most successful scheme of international human rights protection; the aim here is to both identify the moral and legal principles underlying this success and assess their applicability to other human rights schemes (regional and global). This course presupposes a basic understanding of rights theory, political philosophy and international relations theory, and makes use of philosophical texts, legal commentaries, and ECHR decisions.

Required Text(s):
Course Reader

Instructor: Burleigh Wilkins

Philosophy 296D

Seminar in Philosophy of Mind

SH 5617 | R (3:30-6:20pm)

The topics for this seminar will be (a) demonstrative thought, (b) the content of perceptual experience, and (c) the relation between (a) and (b). We will be interested in what conditions must be satisfied in order for a thinker to have a thought that is genuinely about a particular object (as opposed to a thought about whoever or whatever is the so-and-so, for example), and how the perceptual presence of the object in question makes such thoughts available to the thinker. We will read work by Tyler Burge, John Searle, John McDowell, Gareth Evans, Christopher Peacocke, and others. NOTE: Students planning to attend the seminar should read and be prepared to discuss Burge's "Belief /De Re/" /(Journal of //Philosophy/, 1977, available on JSTOR) for the first meeting. Thanks, Kevin.

Required Text(s):
Course Reader

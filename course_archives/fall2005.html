Philosophy 1

Introduction to Philosophy

BUCHN 1910 | MWF 11:00 - 11:50 + Discussion

An introduction to the problems and methods of contemporary philosophy, focusing upon metaphysics and epistemology. We will discuss such questions as: Is there life after death? Does God exist? Do I have a soul distinct from my body? What is the nature of consciousness? Could other forms of intelligent life exist, or is cognition the unique endowment of human beings? Could the external world be radically different than I perceive it to be? Readings drawn from both historical and contemporary sources. We will also discuss philosophically relevant items from popular culture, such as the movies The Matrix, The Truman Show, Blade Runner, and others.

Required Text(s):
Perry, Dialogue on Personal Identity and Immortality
Hofstadter, The Mind's I

Instructor: M. Rescorla

Class TAs: M. Hannen, A. Naraghi

Philosophy 3

Critical Thinking

BRDA 1610 | MWF 10:00 - 10:50 + Discussion

This class is an introduction to logic, that branch of philosophy which is especially concerned with methods for evaluating argumens. We're bombarded with arguments all the time from politicians and the media, and this course will help you in better sorting out the argumentative wheat from chaff. Some of the topics to be covred in this course include: the method of counterexamples, informal fallacies, categorical syllogisms, and basic propositional logic (including truth tables and the method of natural deduction). The basis of grading will be one midterm, a short writing assignment and a (non-cumulative) final.

Required Text(s):
Layman, The Power of Logic, 3rd ed.

Instructor: S. Choi

Class TAs: J. Blair, J. Chandler, M. Grady, K. Schutte

Philosophy 4

Introduction to Ethics

BUCHN 1910 | MWF 1:00 - 1:50 + Discussion

Are there moral facts we can discover? If so, just how do we go about figuring out what is good, right and just? If not, why does it seem to me as though murder is wrong and kindness a virtue? What should I do if I want to act morally? If I don't want to act morally, is there still a sense in which I should act morally? Should I try to make everyone as happy as possible or should I primarily concern myself with helping myself, or my friends and family? Do individuals have absolute rights that I must never transgress, or should I always aim at the good of society as a whole? What would a correct moral theory say about such things as abortion, euthanasia and severe global inequality? Is there any way to resolve disputes over whether such things are moral permissible? These are the questions we'll raise and discuss in Philosophy 4.

Required Text(s):
Kant, Groundwork for the Metaphysic of Morals
Mill, Utilitarianism

Instructor: A. Zimmerman

Class TAs: J. Altschul, S. Huizenga, J. Newman, J.Scott

Philosophy 20A

History of Philosophy

GIRV 1004 | MWF 9:00 - 9:50 + Discussion

The course is focused on methodological, epistemological and ethical issues. For example: the Socratic method, Socratic definition, Plato's theory of Forms and Aristotle's account of the good life.

TBA

Philosophy 100A

Ethics

NH 1006 | MWF 12:00 - 12:50 + Discussion

In this course we will survey the main kinds of normative moral theory through historically important sources. Among the basic questions we will consider are: Why should one be moral? How stringent are the demands of morality? Are there objective truths in morality, or are right and wrong only in the eye of the beholder? Do ends justify the means, or are there some actions that are always wrong, nor matter how much good may result from them?

Required Text(s):
Morgan, Classics of Moral and Political Philosophy

Instructor: M. Hanser

Class TAs: J. Lee, J. Way

Philosophy 100B

Theory of Knowledge

ARTS 1241 | MWF 2:00 - 2:50 + Discussion

If you think about "The Matrix", this should raise the question: Do we know anything at all about the world we are living in? We will discuss this problem of skepticism about knowledge of the external world. We will also discuss the analysis of the concepts of knowledge and justification: what is it to know something and to have rational justification for believing something?

Required Text(s):
TBA

Instructor: A. Brueckner

Class TA: J. Steinberg

Philosophy 100C

Philosophy of Language

PHELP 1160 | TR 11:00 - 12:15 + Discussion

This course is an introduction to some topics in contemporary analytic philosophy of language. We will discuss such questions as: How do words refer to things in the world? What is the relation between what our words mean and what speakers mean in uttering those words? What is the role of truth in the theory of meaning? To what extent is language a social phenomenon? What is the relation between language and thought? The readings for the course will be made available at one of the local copy centers.

Required Text(s):
Reader

Instructor: K. Falvey

Class TA: Tim Lewis

Philosophy 130/230G

Freedom and Determinism

ARTS 1247 | MWF 11:00 - 11:50

Determinism is the doctrine that the laws of nature plus the past necessitate the future: given the laws and the past, only one future is possible. Is determinism compatible with the view that we often act freely? Is determinism compatible with the view that we often are morally responsible for what we do? Is determinism true? Are we free and responsible?

Required Text(s):
Van Inwagen, An Essay on Free Will

Instructor: A. Brueckner

Philosophy 152/252G

Plato

PHELP 1445 | TR 2:00 - 3:15

Plato's early dialogues and the Republic. Topics will include methodological issues such as Socratic definition and the Socratic method; the moral views of the Platonic Socrates, including Socratic paradoxes, Socratic intellectualism, and Socrates' objections to traditional rhetoric and sophistry; and Plato's metaphysics and epistemology as developed in the middle books of the Republic . A principal goal of the course will be to locate the epistemological and ethical issues raised by these dialogues in the context of contemporary philosophical discussion.

Philosophy 183/283G

Beginning Modern/Symbolic Logic

PSYCH 1824 | MWF 10:00 - 10:50 + Discussion

This course is an introduction to the study of correct reasoning--drawing inferences, distinguishing good arguments from bad ones, seeing one's way from assumptions to their consequences. Reasoning is a cognitive process or activity employed in mathematics and other disciplines, as well as in everyday decision making, problem solving, argumentation, and discussion. We shall study the principles and techniques of correct reasoning by developing an artificial symbolic system of "natural deduction," which closely mirrors, but sharpens and systematizes, the patterns of ordinary reasoning that we engage in every day.

Required Text(s):
Kalish, et. al., Logic: Techniques of Formal Reasoning

Instructor: N. Salmon

Class TA: L. Manning

Philosophy 100C

Philosophy of Language

GIRV 1112 | MTWR 2:00-3:15

This course is an introduction to some topics in contemporary analytic philosophy of language. We will discuss such questions as: How do words refer to things in the world? What is the relation between what our words mean and what speakers mean in uttering those words? What is the role of truth in the theory of meaning? To what extent is language a social phenomenon? What is the relation between language and thought? The readings for the course will be made available at one of the local copy centers.

Philosophy 112

Philosophy of Religion

ARTS 1426 | TWR 12:30-1:55

An introductory survey of a number of philosophical issues connected with the concepts and beliefs making up the great monotheistic religious traditions of Judaism, Christianity and Islam. A large portion of the course deals with the concept of God and belief in the existence of God. We will examine what have been historically the most important arguments for the existence of God—the cosmological argument, the argument from design, and the ontological argument—as well as consider the evidential value for religious belief provided by the alleged occurrence of miracles, by the variety of religious or mystical experiences and by the alleged objectivity of morality. Then we will consider grounds and arguments against the existence of God, beginning with alleged incoherencies in the very concept of God and ending with an extensive consideration of what many consider the biggest obstacle to religious belief—the problem of evil and suffering. Finally, we will stand back from our deliberations and ask: in the light of all the arguments pro and con, is belief in God rational? At each stage we will come to grips with some of the best our ancestors have contributed to these issues—works by Plato, Aquinas, Anselm, Descartes, Hume, Kant, Pascal, Freud, James and Russell—as well as important writings of contemporary philosophers. Requirements are: final exam, plus two short papers.

Required Text(s):
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion.
Pojman, Philosophy of Religion.
Course Reader

Instructor: William Forgie | forgie@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Philosophy 20C

History of Philosophy

SH 1430 | MTWR 9:30-10:35

This course will survey an exciting and influential period from roughly 1600 to 1850, when several new areas were developed by philosophers who raised questions that we are still grappling with today. We will look specifically at philosophy of science, philosophy of mind and language, and political philosophy, and how these ideas are still relevant to philosophy and academia in general today. The philosophers covered in the course are Isaac Newton, Gottfried Leibniz, John Locke, David Hume, Thomas Hobbes, Jean-Jaques Rousseau, and Immanuel Kant.

Philosophy 100B

Theory of Knowledge

ARTS 1246 | TWR 12:30-1:55

This is an upper-division introduction to epistemology. Epistemology is that part of philosophy whose main concerns are knowledge and justification. The central questions that we are going to address are: What are the necessary and sufficient conditions that must be satisfied for a subject to know something? What is the structure of knowledge? What are the sources of knowledge? What are the limits of knowledge? What does it mean for someone to be justified in believing something? Is justification for belief internal or external to the mind?

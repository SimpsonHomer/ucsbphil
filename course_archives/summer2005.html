Session A: June 20 - July 29

Philosophy 3

Critical Thinking

PHELP 3523 | MTWR 11:00-12:10

If you do not want to become more logical, you should not take Phil 3. But you want to become more logical. So, you should take Phil 3. If you think that the above argument provides good reason for taking Phil 3, then you were taken in by a bad argument, and hence probably should take Phil 3! Phil 3 is an introductory logic and critical thinking course, logic being the subdiscipline of philosophy which is especially concerned with what makes an argument good or bad. Ability to reason well is a good skill to have for its own sake, and it will also help you avoid getting 'taken in' by bad arguments offered by, for example, politicians and the popular media. Also, many standardized entry exams for graduate or professional schools (e.g. GRE, MCAT, LSAT) have a section testing one's logical and analytic skills. Skills gained through this course will most likely help you do better on such tests than you otherwise would.

Required Text(s):
Layman, The Power of Logic, 3rd ed.

Instructor: S. Choi

Philosophy 7

Biomedical Ethics

PHELP 1425 | MTWR 2:00-3:10

This course will deal with moral controversies that arise in biomedical ethics. The issues to be examined are human cloning, surrogate motherhood, abortion, physician-assisted suicide, euthanasia, human and animal research, organ procurement, lying to patients, decision making for the incompetent, etc. The student's grade for the course will be the result of a short paper (2-3 pages), a midterm exam, a longer paper (4-5 pages), and a cumulative final exam. The course will use a reader which will be available at GrafikArt in Isla Vista. For more information contact the instructor through the philosophy department in South Hall or by email at steinberg@umail.ucsb.edu.

Required Text(s):
Reader, available at GrafikArt

Instructor: J. Steinberg

Philosophy 20B

History of Philosophy

ARTS 1426 | MTWR 9:30-10:40

This course is the second of a three-course series on the history of Western Philosophy. The first half of the course will be devoted to studying the works of a number of important and influential philosophers of the medieval period such as Augustine, Anselm and Thomas Aquinas. Our focus will be their works that address questions such as: Are there rational proofs for the existence of God? How can there be evil in the world if the world is the creation of a benevolent God? Are human freedom and divine foreknowledge compatible? In the second half of the course, we will focus on early modern philosophy as it emerged during and after the scientific revolutions of the 15th and 16th centuries, and the religious and political turmoil left by various events, e.g., the Reformation. Here our focus will be Rene Descartes' Meditations on First Philosophy (a work that defined many of the problems that early modern philosophy was to concern itself with and whose influence on contemporary philosophy is still considerable), and the political philosophy of Thomas Hobbes. Some of the philosophical questions that we will encounter are: Is it possible to know anything with certainty, and if so, how? What is more fundamental in our acquisition of knowledge, reason or experience? What is the nature of the mind, the nature of matter, and how are they related? How ought a society be formed and governed? What grounds and binds citizens to the legal demands of a government? Please contact richg@umail.ucsb.edu for any questions or inquiries.

TBA

Instructor: R. Graziano

Philosophy 100D

Philosophy of Mind

ARTS 1426 | TWR 12:30-1:50

We will study various theoretical accounts of the relation between the mind and the body, including (i) dualism: the view that the mind is an immaterial substance wholly distinct from the body; (ii) behaviorism: mental states are dispositions to behave in various ways; (iii) the mind-brain identity theories: mental states are states of the brain; and (iv) functionalism: mental states are functional states, analogous to the states of a computer running a program. If time permits we will conclude by examining the relationship between commonsense psychology and scientific psychology. There is no text, readings will be made available at one of the local copy centers.

Required Text(s):
Reader

Instructor: K. Falvey

Philosophy 20C

History of Philosophy

SH 1430 | MTWR 9:30-10:40

In this course we'll discuss some of the philosophical developments in the early modern period (c. 1600-1800). We will focus almost exclusively on developments in the philosophy of mind, the theory of knowledge, and metaphysics. Some of the questions that philosophers during this time period were especially interested in and on which we'll concentrate are the following: Where do our ideas come from? Do I have the ability to have ideas about birds, trees, numbers, etc. just because I've had certain experiences, or are some ideas ‚Äúinnate‚Äù (whatever that means)? Is experience the sort of thing that can provide me with ideas about things in the world? If so, then what must experience be like if it's going to provide me with such ideas? What must my ideas be like if they're the sort of thing that I acquire from experience and which are about stuff in the world? What must the world be like if it's the sort of thing I can have thoughts and ideas about? What is it about my thought that eagles are birds that makes it a thought about eagles and not about something else? How is it possible not only to have ideas about the world by means of experience, but also to have knowledge about the world? Can I have knowledge about the world? We will focus on the works of G. W. Leibniz, John Locke, George Berkeley, David Hume, and Immanuel Kant. From time to time we may compare the answers to the above questions given by these and other philosophers of the early modern period with the answers given by contemporary (i.e., living) philosophers.

Required Text(s):
Reader. Hume, An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding

Instructor: D. Dodd

Philosophy 112

Philosophy of Religion

ARTS 1245 | TWR 12:30-1:50

An introductory survey of a number of philosophical issues connected with the concepts and beliefs making up the great monotheistic religious traditions of Judaism, Christianity and Islam. A large portion of the course deals with the concept of God and belief in the existence of God. We will examine what have been historically the most important arguments for the existence of God—the cosmological argument, the argument from design, and the ontological argument—as well as consider the evidential value for religious belief provided by the alleged occurrence of miracles, by the variety of religious or mystical experiences and by the alleged objectivity of morality. Then we will consider grounds and arguments against the existence of God, beginning with alleged incoherencies in the very concept of God and ending with an extensive consideration of what many consider the biggest obstacle to religious belief—the problem of evil and suffering. Finally, we will stand back from our deliberations and ask: in the light of all the arguments pro and con, is belief in God rational? At each stage we will come to grips with some of the best our ancestors have contributed to these issues—works by Plato, Aquinas, Anselm, Descartes, Hume, Kant, Pascal, Freud, James and Russell—as well as important writings of contemporary philosophers. Requirements are: final exam, plus two short papers.

Required Text(s):
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion. Pojman, Philosophy of Religion. Reader

Instructor: J. W. Forgie

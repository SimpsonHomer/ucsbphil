Philosophy 1

Introduction to Philosophy

BUCHN 1910 | MWF (10:00-10:50am) + Discussion

We will consider some fundamental questions of philosophy: "What is the relation between a person's mind and their body?", "What do we really know about the physical world and its laws?", "Are ethical claims subjective or are there objective moral truths—if so, what are they?", "Is there a God?". We will read selections from both classical and modern philosophers.

Required Text(s):
Steven M. Cahn (ed.), Philosophy for the 21st Century: A Comprehensive Reader, New York & Oxford, Oxford University Press, 2003.
Thomas Nagel, What Does It All Mean?

Instructor: C. Anthony Anderson | caanders@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Course TAs: M. Grady, L. Manning, K. Schutte, J. Way

Philosophy 3

Critical Thinking

BUCHN 1920 | MWF (9:00-9:50am) + Discussion

If you do not want to become more logical, you should not take Phil 3. But you want to become more logical. So, you should take Phil 3. If you think that the above argument provides good reason for taking Phil 3, then you were taken in by a bad argument, and hence probably should take Phil 3! Phil 3 is an introductory logic and critical thinking course, logic being the subdiscipline of philosophy which is especially concerned with what makes an argument good or bad. Ability to reason well is a good skill to have for its own sake, and it will also help you avoid getting 'taken in' by bad arguments offered by, for example, politicians and the popular media. Also, many standardized entry exams for graduate or professional schools (e.g. GRE, MCAT, LSAT) have a section testing one's logical and analytic skills. Skills gained through this course will most likely help you do better on such tests than you otherwise would.

Required Text(s):
Layman, The Power of Logic, 3rd ed.

Instructor: Sean Choi | seanc@umail.ucsb.edu

Course TAs: J. Altschul, J. Blair

Philosophy 4

Introduction to Ethics

EMBAR HALL 1910 | MWF (11:00-11:50am) + Discussion

This course is concerned with three different types of moral questions: (1) Questions about the nature of moral principles and moral judgments, and whether and how we can get knowledge of them. Questions like: what do we mean by calling something "morally justified", or "good", or "right", etc. (2) Questions about the very general moral principles behind moral judgments, and moral decisions: What principles ought we to live by? What are the basic principles of right and wrong? What makes someone a good person?, etc. (3) Questions about specific moral issues: Is abortion morally permissible? Is there a moral requirement for the affluent to give money to prevent the suffering and death of impoverished people?, etc.

Required Text(s):
James Rachels, The Elements of Moral Philosophy.
James Rachels, The Right Thing to Do

Instructor: Arash Naraghi | anaraghi@umail.ucsb.edu

Course TAs: C. Barnes, D. Dodd, C. Evans, M. Hannen, H. Salazar, J. Scott

Philosophy 7

Biomedical Ethics

SH 1431 | MWF (12:00-12:50pm) + Discussion

This course will deal with moral controversies that arise in biomedical ethics. Philosophy 20B

History of Philosophy: Medievals-Rationalists

IV THEA-1 | MWF (1:00-1:50pm) + Discussion

Does God exist? How can evil exist if there is a benevolent, all powerful God? Can we know anything? Do we have free will? These are some of the questions we will explore in this section of a three course series on the history of Western philosophy. We will start with pre-medieval and medieval philosophers such as St. Augustine, St. Anselm and St. Thomas Aquinas. Next, we will briefly look at the scientific revolution of the 16th and 17th centuries and move on to some early modern thinkers who addressed the philosophical problems which became predominant in the wake of this revolution. We will closely examine Descartes' Meditations and read selections from works of Spinoza and Leibniz.

Philosophy 100D

Philosophy of Mind

PHELP 3505 | TR (11:00-12:15pm) + Discussion

Humans can think, doubt, yearn, and regret. Rocks can do none of these things. This is because humans have minds, while rocks do not. But why are we so confident that humans have minds and rocks do not? What about intermediate cases like dolphins, dogs, or trees? Even if I am sure that I have a mind, why should I be so sure that you do? What does it even mean to say that a creature has a mind? Does it mean that the creature has something like an immaterial soul? Does it mean that, unlike a rock, the creature’s behavior is complicated enough to warrant attributing mental activity to it? Does it mean that the creature has a sufficiently complex brain? We will study the various ways in which philosophers have tried to answer these questions during the past century.

Philosophy 112/212G

Philosophy of Religion

HSSB 1173 | MWF (1:00-1:50pm)

An introductory survey of a number of philosophical issues connected with the concepts and beliefs making up the great monotheistic religious traditions of Judaism, Christianity and Islam. A large portion of the course deals with the concept of God and belief in the existence of God. We will examine what have been historically the most important arguments for the existence of God—the cosmological argument, the argument from design, and the ontological argument—as well as consider the evidential value for religious belief provided by the alleged occurrence of miracles, by the variety of religious or mystical experiences and by the alleged objectivity of morality. Then we will consider grounds and arguments against the existence of God, beginning with alleged incoherencies in the very concept of God and ending with an extensive consideration of what many consider the biggest obstacle to religious belief—the problem of evil and suffering. Finally, we will stand back from our deliberations and ask: in the light of all the arguments pro and con, is belief in God rational? At each stage we will come to grips with some of the best our ancestors have contributed to these issues—works by Plato, Aquinas, Anselm, Descartes, Hume, Kant, Pascal, Freud, James and Russell—as well as important writings of contemporary philosophers. Requirements: final exam, plus two short papers.

Required Text(s):
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion.
Pojman, Philosophy of Religion.
Course Reader.

Instructor: William Forgie | forgie@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Philosophy 124/224G

Philosophy of Science

PHELP 3515 | TR (12:30-1:45pm)

"Philosophical concern with the nature of space and time is as old as philosophy itself. The mathematical and physical developments of the scientific progress since the seventeenth-century scientific revolution have made this concern take on quite new aspects, however. The philosopher can no longer take the nature of space and time as a pretty much self-evident given, assuming that this nature and our knowledge of it requires philosophical clarification, perhaps, but is in itself a definite, unchanging given to him. For with the advent of non-Euclidean geometries, their application to physical theories, the relativistic revisions in our concept of time, etc., the nature of space and time, or better, spacetime, has become itself a matter of scientific and philosophical dispute," quoted from the introduction to Space, Time and Spacetime.

Philosophy 131/231G

Advanced Topics in Applied Ethics

BLD 387 #104 | MWF (12:30-1:45am)

This course will address various ethical issues that arise in our thinking about matters of war and of peace. We will examine question such as the following: Is morality applicable to international relations? When (if ever) are we morally justified in going to war? What moral constraints (if any) should we observe while prosecuting a war? Who counts as a combatant and who counts as a noncombatant? Can terrorism ever be justified? How should we react to terrorism? When (if ever) are we justified in resorting to strategic bombing? To blockades and sanctions regimes? Who is responsible for war and war crimes? Is there such a thing as collective responsibility?

Required Text(s):
Lackey, The Ethics of War and Peace.
Walzer, Just and Unjust Wars.

Instructor: Thomas Holden | tholden@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Philosophy 150D/250D

Advanced Topics in Philosophy of Mind

SH 5617 | TR (9:30-10:45am)

In this course we will study a family of "neo-dualist" arguments which purport to show in different ways that certain features of our conscious mental life cannot be reduced to or identified with physical facts or properties. The arguments include Thomas Nagel's famous argument that we cannot know what it is like to be a bat, Saul Kripke's argument that it is conceivable that pain is not a brain state (and this conceiving cannot be explained away in certain ways), Frank Jackson's argument that knowledge of all the physical facts concering colors does not convey knowledge of what it is like to see redness, and David Chalmers argument(s) that zombies--beings who are phyiscally, functionally, and behaviorally similar to humans but who have no conscious menta life--are conceivable, and hence possible.

Required Text(s):
John Perry, Knowledge, Possibility, and Consciousness, MIT Press, 2003

Instructor: Kevin Falvey | falvey@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Philosophy 160/260G

Descartes

HSSB 1214 | MWF (11:00-11:50am)

It has been said that much of the 20th century philosophy can be seen as an attempt to break the hold of Descartes on our philosophical sensibilities. Whatever the truth of that, there can be no doubt of Descartes’ enormous influence on the agenda of the past century—e.g., the centrality of epistemology to philosophy, the obsession with skepticism, the mind/body problem (or problems), the nature of the self. Perhaps no other past philosopher except Hume is still so much with us. In this course we will examine Descartes’ conception of the task of philosophy as well as his epistemology, metaphysics and philosophy of mind. We will also look at some illustrative, and more contemporary, alternatives to the Cartesianism.

Required Text(s):
Descartes, The Philosophical Writings, Vol. I.
Descartes, The Philosophical Writings, Vol. II.

Instructor: William Forgie | forgie@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Philosophy 187/287G

Philosophy of Mathematics

BUCHN 1934 | TR (2:00-3:15pm)

An introduction to the three major historical schools of philosophy of mathematics: logicism, intuitionism, and formalism. We will address such questions as: What is the nature of number? For instance, what is the number one? What is the nature of infinity? Is there a notion of mathematical truth distinct from what is provable? Does mathematics describe an objective realm independent of human cognition, or are mathematical entities and facts somehow a product of our own thinking? Given that we discover mathematical truths through a priori reasoning, what explains the astounding applicability of mathematics to the physical universe?

Required Text(s):
Alexander George and Daniel Velleman, Philosophies of Mathematics.
Paul Benacerraf and Hilary Putnam, Philosophy of Mathematics.
**Prerequisite: Some familiarity with deductive logic, or some background in mathematics.

Instructor: Michael Rescorla | rescorla@philosophy.ucsb.edu

Philosophy 1

Introduction to Philosophy

ENGR1 1104 | MWF 11:00 - 11:50 + Discussion

An introduction to the problems and methods of contemporary philosophy, focusing upon metaphysics and epistemology. We will discuss such questions as: Is there life after death? Does God exist? Do I have a soul distinct from my body? What is the nature of consciousness? Could other forms of intelligent life exist, or is cognition the unique endowment of human beings? Could the external world be radically different than I perceive it to be? Readings drawn from both historical and contemporary sources. We will also discuss philosophically relevant items from popular culture, such as the movies The Matrix, The Truman Show, Blade Runner, and others.

Required Text(s):
Perry, Dialogue on Personal Identity and Immortality. Hofstadter, The Mind's I

Instructor: M. Rescorla

Philosophy 4

Introduction to Ethics

BUCHN 1910 | MWF 1:00 - 1:50 + Discussion

Are there moral facts we can discover? If so, just how do we go about figuring out what is good, right and just? If not, why does it seem to me as though murder is wrong and kindness a virtue? What should I do if I want to act morally? If I don't want to act morally, is there still a sense in which I should act morally? Should I try to make everyone as happy as possible or should I primarily concern myself with helping myself, or my friends and family? Do individuals have absolute rights that I must never transgress, or should I always aim at the good of society as a whole? What would a correct moral theory say about such things as abortion, euthanasia and severe global inequality? Is there any way to resolve disputes over whether such things are moral permissible? These are the questions we'll raise and discuss in Philosophy 4.

Required Text(s):
Kant, Groundwork for the Metaphysic of Morals. Mill, Utilitarianism

Instructor: A. Zimmerman

Philosophy 20A

History of Philosophy

GIRV 1004 | MWF 9:00 - 9:50 + Discussion

This is an introductory course examining the philosophical positions central to the thought of Socrates, Plato and Aristotle. The course is focused on methodological, epistemological and ethical issues. For example: the Socratic method, Socratic definition, Plato's theory of Forms and Aristotle's account of the good life.

Required Text(s):
Plato (translated by Grube), Plato: Five Dialogues. Guthrie, Socrates. Urmson, Aristotle's Ethics. Hussey, Presocratics. Aristotle, Niomachean Ethic

Instructor: V. Tsouna

Philosophy 100A

Ethics

PHELP 1260 | MWF 12:00 - 12:50 + Discussion

In this course we will survey the main kinds of normative moral theory through historically important sources. Among the basic questions we will consider are: Why should one be moral? How stringent are the demands of morality? Are there objective truths in morality, or are right and wrong only in the eye of the beholder? Do ends justify the means, or are there some actions that are always wrong, nor matter how much good may result from them?

Required Text(s):
Morgan, Classics of Moral and Political Philosophy

Instructor: M. Hanser

Philosophy 100F

Introduction to Philosophy of Science

GIRV 2128 | MWF 2:00 - 2:50 + Discussion

Science enjoys enormous prestige in contemporary society. Television commercials frequently declare that some fact has been "scientifically proved." But do scientists ever conclusively prove anything? Aren't they just as biased and fallible as everyone else? Do scientists discover objective features of reality, or do they merely impose their interpretations upon the data? Do electrons really exist, or are they mere theoretical constructs invented by scientists? In short, is science objectively true? We will investigate how various philosophers have approached these questions during the past century.
This course counts towards the 36 UD units required for the upper-division major. Contact Meredith Sedgwick, the undergraduate advisor, for more information.

Required Text(s):
Godfrey-Smith, Theory and Reality
Kuhn, The Structure of Scientific Revolutions
Reader

Instructor: M. Rescorla

Philosophy 112/212G

Philosophy of Religion

PSYCH 1802 | TR 11:00 - 12:15

An introductory survey of a number of philosophical issues connected with the concepts and beliefs making up the great monotheistic religious traditions of Judaism, Christianity and Islam. A large portion of the course deals with the concept of God and belief in the existence of God. We will examine what have been historically the most important arguments for the existence of God—the cosmological argument, the argument from design, and the ontological argument—as well as consider the evidential value for religious belief provided by the alleged occurrence of miracles, by the variety of religious or mystical experiences and by the alleged objectivity of morality. Then we will consider grounds and arguments against the existence of God, beginning with alleged incoherencies in the very concept of God and ending with an extensive consideration of what many consider the biggest obstacle to religious belief—the problem of evil and suffering. Finally, we will stand back from our deliberations and ask: in the light of all the arguments pro and con, is belief in God rational? At each stage we will come to grips with some of the best our ancestors have contributed to these issues—works by Plato, Aquinas, Anselm, Descartes, Hume, Kant, Pascal, Freud, James and Russell—as well as important writings of contemporary philosophers. Requirements are: final exam, plus two short papers.

Required Text(s):
Pojman, Philosophy of Religion: An Anthology
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion

Instructor: J. W. Forgie

Philosophy 116/216G

Meaning and Reference

SH 5617 | TR 12:30-1:45

An examination of the classical theories of meaning and reference: John Stuart Mill, Gottlob Frege, Bertrand Russell, Saul Kripke, and the theory of direct reference. Issues to be discussed center on the following: what is the nature of the information content of a declarative sentence, and what do the constituents of the sentence contribute? How is the referent of a term secured? How is the information content of a sentence secured, and how is the contribution of the sentence constituents secured? Solutions to the classical puzzles of meaning and reference are investigated.

Required Text(s):
Garfield, Meaning and Truth
Salmon, Frege's Puzzle
Optional Text: Martinich, Philosophy of Language
Kripke, Naming and Necessity
Seech, Writing Philosophy Papers

Instructor: N. Salmon

Philosophy 156/256G

Hellenistic Philosophy

PHELP 1445 | MWF 12:00 - 12:50

The course examines the philosophies of the Stoics, the Epicureans, and the Skeptics, as they developed during the last three centuries B.C. It will consider the metaphysical, epistemological, and ethical views of the Stoics and Epicureans, and will trace the development of the methods of the Skeptics, and will discuss the philosophical interaction among these schools. Particular emphasis will be given to the relevance to contemporary philosophical thought of Hellenistic concepts such as rationalism, empiricism, the criteria of truth, the utilitarian calculus, and virtue ethics.

Required Text(s):
Long and Sedley, Hellenistic Philosophers
Long, Hellenistic Philosophy

Instructor: V. Tsouna

Philosophy 165/265G

Hume

GIRV 2127 | MWF 10:00 - 10:50

This course will examine A Treatise of Human Nature, an important work by the influential eighteenth century philosopher David Hume. We'll look critically at Hume's discussion in that treatise of the nature of mentality, causation, space, time, free will and morality. We'll concentrate on the original text, but we may also look at Don Garrett's recent study Cognition and Commitment in Hume's Philosophy.

Required Text(s):
Hume, A Treatise of Human Nature
Garrett, Cognition and Commitment in Hume's Philosophy

Instructor: A. Zimmerman

Philosophy 183/283G

Beginning Modern/Symbolic Logic

WEBB 1100 | TR 11:00 - 12:15 + Discussion

This course is an introduction to the study of correct reasoning--drawing inferences, distinguishing good arguments from bad ones, seeing one's way from assumptions to their consequences. Reasoning is a cognitive process or activity employed in mathematics and other disciplines, as well as in everyday decision making, problem solving, argumentation, and discussion. We shall study the principles and techniques of correct reasoning by developing an artificial symbolic system of "natural deduction," which closely mirrors, but sharpens and systematizes, the patterns of ordinary reasoning that we engage in every day.

Required Text(s):
Kalish, et. al., Logic: Techniques of Formal Reasoning

Instructor: N. Salmon

Philosophy 297A

Seminar in History of Philosophy

SH 5617 | R 3:30 - 6:20

This course will examine the philosophy of David Hume (1711-76), paying special attention to his metaphysics and epistemology. We will also cover areas such as philosophy of mind, metaethics, and his philosophy of religion insofar as these fall under 'metaphysics and epistemology' broadly construed. There won't be much attention to his practical philosophy (such as his first-order ethics, political philosophy and economics) however. We will focus primarily on Hume's major philosophical works, the Treatise, the Enquiry Concerning Natural Religion, aiming first to understand his theories, second to track down their weaknesses, and third to examine how some of his ideas played out (and continue to play out) in later philosophical epochs.

Required Text(s):
Hume, Treatise of Human Nature
Hume, Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding
Hume, Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion

Instructor: T. Holden

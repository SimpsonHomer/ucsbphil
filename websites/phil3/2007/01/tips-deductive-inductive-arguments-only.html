Tips: Deductive - inductive arguments, "Only if"

1. Deductive Arguments vs Inductive Arguments

Although we mainly deal with deductive arguments in this course, it might be helpful to be able to distinguish deductive arguments from inductive arguments. Here’s one way to distinguish the two:

In a deductive argument, the premises are claimed to necessitate the conclusion.

Another way of saying it is:

In a deductive argument, the premises are claimed to support the conclusion in such a way that it is necessary that if the premises are true, then the conclusion is true.

On the other hand,

In an inductive argument, the premises are claimed to support the conclusion in such a way that it is likely (or probable) that if the premises are true, then the conclusion is true.

Consider the following argument:

PR1. 90% of Americans have watched at least one episode of Seinfeld’s.

PR2. Josh is an American

Con. Therefore, Josh has watched at least one episode of Seinfeld’s.

Although the premises do not guarantee the truth of the conclusion, the conclusion is likely to follow from the premises. In fact, in presenting this argument, the arguer would like to claim that it is only likely that the conclusion follows from the premises. He would not claim that the conclusion necessarily follows from the premises. In this sense, the given argument is an inductive argument.

2. “If … then…” & “… only if …”

Benedicte has emphasized that “P only if Q” is equivalent to “If P, then Q”.

Consider the following statements:

1. You can enter the theater only if you’re over 17.
2. If you can enter the theater, then you’re over 17.
3. If you’re over 17, then you can enter the theater.

According to what Benedicte said, (1) should be equivalent to (2), not to (3). But perhaps one might think that it is (3) that is equivalent to (1). That’s wrong, though. And here’s why:

In saying (1), you’re saying that being over 17 is necessary for you to enter that theater. In other words, you gotta be over 17 to enter that theater. However, does (1) say that being over 17 is all it takes for you to enter the theater? No. For instance, if you don’t have the ticket, then you can’t enter the theater (even if you’re over 17). Think of it like this way:

Here are the list of the things that are required for you to enter the theater:

* being over 17
* admission ticket
* free time

…..

What (1) says is just that being over 17 is one requirement for you to enter the theater. However, that doesn’t mean that being over 17 is the only thing that is required for you to enter the theater. You must have the ticket, you must have finished other things, say, homework (so you have free time to watch movie), you must be not terribly sick, etc. In sum, even if you meet that condition (i.e. even if you’re over 17), that doesn’t guarantee you to enter the theater. However, according to (3), being over 17 does guarantee you to enter the theater. Hence, (3) is actually false. (If (3) is true, then it would mean that anyone who is over 17 can watch movies for free!)

On the other hand, what (2) says is: “If you’re not over 17, then you can’t enter the theater.” This is exactly what (1) says. Thus, (1) is equivalent to (2), but not to (3).
